I am a doctoral student in Political Science at Washington University in St. Louis, studying Political Methodology and International Relations.

I am passionate about using Bayesian Analysis to leverage the rich complexities of social sciences data in pursuit of solving the fundamental puzzles of our international political system, particularly international interventions, state-building, and the United Nations.

Before pursuing my PhD at Washington University, I earned a Master’s in Quantitative Methods in the Social Sciences (QMSS) at Columbia University, where my thesis used Bayesian analysis to study how international institutions adapt after endogenous shocks; a Master’s in International Relations at the University of Chicago, where my thesis explored how authoritarian regimes can make credible commitments; and a Bachelor’s with interdisciplinary honors in International Relations at Stanford University, where my thesis presented original research on the United Nation’s efforts to promote democracy around the world.

Outside of academia, I have extensive experience working as an analyst for the healthcare sector, working as a consultant to the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid and an analyst at Kaiser Permanente where I supported surgical services.

Working Papers

Grass-roots Statebuilding in Post-conflict States

State-building endeavors usually focus on top-down institution building, which have had mixed success and are controversial. What happens when instead state-building is from the bottom-up? I examine this question using original data on a United Nations state-building project in post-civil war Nepal. This is my job market paper and the first chapter of my dissertation.

Can International Interventions Build States? Evidence from the United Nations Development Programme

Using an original dataset with geolocated UNDP projects and project expenditures, I seek to arbitrate a debate on whether international interventions can effectively build institutions and mitigate violence in failed and fragile states.

A Multi-Task Gaussian Process Model for Inferring Time-Varying Treatment Effects in Panel Data

In collaboration with Dr. Jacob Montgomery, Dr. Roman Garnett, and Yehu Chen, I am building with Matlab's gmpl software a Gaussian Process approach to the difference-in-differences model that relaxes the parallel trends and linearity assumptions.

Modelling Cycles of Violence in Israel and Palestine

In collaboration with Dr. Carly Wayne, I am building a causally identified time series model that captures the cycles of violence using an original, fine-grained event dataset.

Foreign Aid and Violence in Mali

In collaboration with Dr. Will Nomikos and Dr. Allison Namias Grossman, I am examining how elites in Mali view international interventions using an original survey of semi-structured interviews.
